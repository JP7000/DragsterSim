Rolling Resistance
▶
Rolling resistance coefficient (μr) captures energy lost as wheels flex and deform against the track. Acts as a constant force throughout the run: F_roll = μr × m × g. Smooth aluminium gives the lowest values; jointed sheet-metal adds significant penalty.
Dimensionless rolling resistance coefficient. Typical CO₂ dragster range: 0.006–0.025. Lower = faster. Standard competition value on aluminium track is ~0.010.

What's slowing your car down? explain these forces
At launch the car is stationary so air drag is zero. All resistance comes from wheel spin-up — the rotational inertia of the wheels acting as additional effective mass. PLA wheels are modelled as annular discs: I = ½m(R_outer² + R_bore²), so I/R² = ½m(1 + (R_bore/R)²). A larger bore increases the penalty above the solid-disc baseline.

Rolling resistance acts as a constant force throughout the entire run: F_roll = μr × m(t) × g. It decreases slightly as the CO₂ cartridge empties and total mass falls. This is the primary variable you control through wheel-track surface choice.

At peak speed air drag dominates — it grows with the square of velocity and becomes the single largest resistive force the car fights at high speed. This is where aerodynamic body shaping and frontal area reduction pay the biggest dividends.
